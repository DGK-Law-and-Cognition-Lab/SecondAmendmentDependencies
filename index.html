Study 1A

Key Findings

We constructed 20 sentences mirroring the Second Amendment's grammatical structure but using politically neutral content. Each participant read one sentence and provided forced-choice interpretations, open-ended rewrites, confidence ratings, and clause-manipulation judgments.

Finding 1
The Categorical Reading Is the Default

90.5% of participants interpreted "being necessary" constructions as categorical assertions: both the necessity and the right always hold. GLMM intercept = 2.44 log-odds, p < .0001.

Finding 2
Nobody Writes Conditionally

When asked to rewrite the sentence in their own words, not a single participant (0/100) produced conditional language. Even the 9 participants who chose "conditional" on the forced-choice question wrote categorically.

Finding 3
The Prefatory Clause Is Contextual

Removing the prefatory clause produces only modest perceived change (M = 3.22, below the "neither similar nor different" midpoint of 4). Readers treat the clause as context, not as a condition.

Finding 4
Politics Don't Predict the Parse

Gun control attitudes do not predict interpretation (logistic regression, p = .25). The categorical reading is not driven by political orientation. Age and gender also show no effect on interpretation.

Ethics

This research was approved by the UCLA Institutional Review Board. All participants provided informed consent. No identifying information beyond Prolific IDs is collected.

About This Research

This project is part of the Law and Cognition Lab's research on how ordinary readers process legal language. The central finding is that the "ordinary meaning" of the Second Amendment's syntactic structure aligns with the categorical reading adopted in Heller, and that this reading is independent of political orientation.
