# Two Centuries of Debate Over a Sentence That Readers Find Unambiguous

**David G. Kamper**, **Anna Walburger**, and **Idan Blank**

University of California, Los Angeles | University of Chicago Law School

---

## The Question

The Second Amendment's twenty-seven words have been debated since ratification. At the center of the debate is a syntactic question: does the prefatory clause ("A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State") limit the operative clause ("the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed"), or does it merely provide context?

This question drove *District of Columbia v. Heller* (2008), *New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen* (2022), and *United States v. Rahimi* (2024). Yet no empirical study has asked how ordinary readers actually interpret the syntax.

## Our Approach

We constructed 20 sentences that mirror the Second Amendment's grammatical structure but use politically neutral content:

> *"A properly organized marketplace, being necessary for economic exchange, the right of people to sell and trade goods, must not be revoked."*

By holding the syntactic structure constant and varying the topic, we isolate how readers process the *form* of the Amendment from how they feel about its *content*.

## Key Findings (Study 1A, N = 100)

**90.5% of readers adopt the categorical interpretation.** When asked what the sentence means, the vast majority treat "being necessary" as stating a fact, not setting a condition. The right is always protected. This replicates our pilot study (84.3%, N = 121) and is robust across all 20 sentence topics.

**Not a single participant produced conditional language in their own words.** When asked to rewrite the sentence without being given interpretive options, zero participants framed the right as contingent on the necessity condition. Participants who chose "conditional" on the forced-choice question wrote categorically when left to their own devices.

**Removing the prefatory clause changes little.** Categorical interpreters rated the implications of removing the "being necessary" clause as more similar than different (*M* = 3.22, below the scale midpoint). The prefatory clause is contextual, not definitional.

**Gun control attitudes do not predict interpretation.** The categorical reading is not driven by political orientation. Pro-restriction and anti-restriction participants read the syntax the same way.

## Implications

For textualist constitutional interpretation, these results establish that the "ordinary meaning" of the Second Amendment's syntactic structure aligns with the individual-rights reading adopted in *Heller*. The perceived ambiguity at the heart of two centuries of legal debate may reflect the structure of that debate rather than the structure of the language.
